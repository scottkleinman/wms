Thursday 24 January 2013 09.42 EST	Research in brief – 24 January 2013	Our weekly round-up aimed at showcasing the latest research across the subject spectrum at UK universities	Experts from around the world believe that plain packaging of tobacco products would cut smoking, a new study by the University of Cambridge has found.; A major study led by a history lecturer from De Montfort University shows how sport helped break anti-Semitic barriers in the 19th and 20th centuries.; Use of the Nintendo Wii Fit could help improve the development of children with movement difficulties, according to a research collaboration between two NHS Trusts and academics at Goldsmiths College and Oxford Brookes University.; Researchers at Imperial College London have shown that keeping healthy blood cells alive could be a more important tool in the fight against leukaemia than keeping cancerous cells at bay.; A team at the University of Manchester's John Rylands Library have discovered designs and engravings by one of Britain's greatest poets and artists – William Blake.; An historian at the University of Southampton has found that the practice of ransom was widespread among all soldiers during the Hundred Years War (1337 – 1453) and not, as generally thought, just the preserve of kings, knights and higher orders.; Researchers from Queen Mary, University of London have created a novel re-identification method that predicts a person's movements in invisible areas using a combination of behavioural models and floor plans. The new model could be a useful security tool in predicting where people move in CCTV blind spots.; Would you like your university's research featured on the network?
